Liminal Vocab · April 2026

Everything's changing
and we don't have
the words.

An open, curated, multilingual cartography of the new vocabulary emerging at the edges of culture — where communities of practice are already naming things the rest of us can't yet see.

The situation

We are living through a liminal time, and our language is behind.

The old vocabulary — democracy, freedom, community, work — has been hollowed out through overuse and political corruption. The new vocabulary that could describe what's actually happening doesn't exist yet. We reach for words that aren't there.

This is not a metaphor. Cognitive scientists like Lera Boroditsky have shown that when we lack language for a phenomenon, we don't just struggle to communicate it — we struggle to think about it at the most basic level. To remember it. To assign responsibility for it.

New realities are emerging, but they're still insubstantial. The old words are corrupted, and the new words don't exist yet. We're caught in between. — Kinsey Carlson, "Everything's changing and we don't have the words"

But Carlson's essay also points to where the new language is already being made: at the edges, in communities of practice — groups of people living inside arrangements so new that they have no choice but to invent the words for them.

The proposal

Map the edges. Document what's being named. Make it legible to everyone else.

A public repository of emerging terms, each one documented not as a dictionary entry but as a small act of observation: where it comes from, what it makes visible, and what kind of shift it performs. No fixed categories — the patterns emerge from the terms themselves.

The method

The form is part of the argument.

The repository borrows its workflow from software development — pull requests, version history, public review — and applies it to humanistic work. That's deliberate. A community of practice (coders) developed a vocabulary for collaborative knowledge work that the humanities never adopted. Using it here is itself an act of translation across edges.

01

Human submission only

Terms are proposed by people who encountered them in lived practice, with a concrete usage citation. No machine-generated entries — not for quality reasons, but because the project studies words born of experience.

02

Public review

Every proposal is a pull request. Discussion, editorial decisions, and even rejections remain visible. The threshold between "not yet ready" and "accepted" is itself part of the cartography.

03

Human-authored analysis

AI can assist with research, translation, and pattern-finding. But the interpretive essay accompanying each entry is always written and signed by a human editor. The machine distills; the human judges.

04

Open, multilingual, navigable

Structured metadata makes the archive filterable by community, era, or pattern — so readers can see, for instance, all the terms that break a false binary into a spectrum. Untranslatability is treated as material, not as a gap to paper over.

Contribute

You know a word that doesn't exist in mainstream language yet? Submit it.

This cartography grows through observation, not invention. If you've encountered a term in a community of practice that names something previously invisible — something the rest of us don't have words for yet — propose it here.

Every submission requires a concrete citation from actual usage. Not paraphrased, not invented. If you can't point to someone using the term in practice, it's not yet what we study.

Which community, practice, or context coined or uses this term?
What phenomenon does this term name that was previously unnamed?
Optional. How does it change our thinking? e.g., "makes invisible work visible", "breaks a binary into a spectrum", "reframes a deficit as a difference"
A concrete quote showing this term being used in practice, with source.
Optional: when did the term emerge? What older term does it displace?
Optional. Will be credited on the proposal.
Or, if you prefer: submit directly as a GitHub Issue.

This project is a direct response to Kinsey Carlson's essay "Everything's changing and we don't have the words", published on In the Making in April 2026. It tries to turn her closing question — which communities of practice are you paying attention to? — into an actual method.

It is early. It is open to collaborators, critics, and especially to people already living inside arrangements that don't have names yet.
