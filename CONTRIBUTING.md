# Contributing to Liminal Vocab

## How to Propose a Term

### Via the Website
Visit [workspace.tricksal.com/liminal-vocab](https://workspace.tricksal.com/liminal-vocab/) and use the submission form.

### Via GitHub
Open an issue using the [Term Proposal template](https://github.com/tricksal/liminal-vocab/issues/new?template=term-proposal.yml).

## What Makes a Good Proposal

Every submission needs four things:

1. **The term:** the word or phrase itself
2. **The community:** where does it come from?
3. **A concrete citation:** a real quote from actual usage, with a source. Not paraphrased, not invented.
4. **What it makes visible:** what phenomenon does this term name that was previously unnamed?

### Why the Citation Requirement?

This isn't bureaucracy. It's a substantive threshold: if you can't point to a concrete instance of the term being used in practice, it's not yet what the project studies. We document language *as it's actually being used*, not language as it could theoretically exist.

## What We Don't Accept

- **AI-generated submissions.** The rationale is methodological: we study terms born of lived experience in communities. A machine has no community and no pressure to make the unsayable sayable.
- **Invented terms.** We document, we don't prescribe.
- **Terms without evidence.** No citation, no entry.

## The Review Process

1. Your proposal appears as a GitHub Issue
2. The editorial team reviews: Is the citation authentic? Is the community plausible?
3. Discussion happens publicly on the issue
4. Decision: accepted, needs changes, or archived with reason
5. Archived proposals remain publicly visible. The in-between is material, not waste

## AI as Editorial Tool

AI may be used in the editorial process as a research instrument: finding additional citations, pattern recognition, or translation help. But interpretive and analytical work is always human-authored and signed.
